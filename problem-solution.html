Project Background

From Area-based to Data-based Farming

This page explains the problem in conventional farming, why a change is needed, and how modern technologies provide a solution. It also highlights the expected impact on farmers, government and the environment.

1. Problem: Conventional Farming Practices

In many regions, farmers follow area-centric practices. The same crops are grown in a large area and the same irrigation and fertilizer plan is used for all farmers. This leads to:

  • Overuse or underuse of water, fertilizers and pesticides.
  • Unpredictable yield and frequent crop losses.
  • Low ability to respond to climate variability and extreme weather.
  • Little or no real-time information about soil health.
  • Higher production cost and low profit margins for small and marginal farmers.

In short: decisions are based on experience and guesswork, not on field data.

2. Why Change is Needed

Soil type, moisture, nutrients and micro-climate can change every few metres in a field. Using the same prescription for all fields wastes resources and reduces productivity.

  • Farmers need field-level data to take accurate decisions for each plot.
  • Government needs reliable data for planning schemes, crop insurance and subsidies.
  • Climate change demands more efficient and sustainable practices.
  • Market demand is shifting towards quality produce with traceability.

The solution is to move from “one-size-fits-all” to data-driven, site-specific farming.

3. Proposed Solution: Precision Agriculture

Our solution is to apply precision agriculture using modern technologies: IoT sensors, drones, satellites, mobile apps, cloud and smart irrigation systems.

  • Collect real-time data from the field (soil moisture, NPK, temperature, humidity).
  • Combine field data with satellite images and weather forecasts.
  • Analyze data using cloud analytics and AI-based models.
  • Provide field-specific advisory to each farmer via mobile app (in local language).
  • Automate actions like irrigation and fertigation based on data-driven rules.

The platform acts as a digital farming assistant that guides the farmer throughout the crop cycle – from sowing to harvest.

4. Objectives of the Project

  • Increase crop yield and quality through better, timely decisions.
  • Reduce input costs for farmers (water, fertilizers, pesticides, labour).
  • Ensure sustainable use of natural resources (soil, water, energy).
  • Enable government departments to use accurate data for planning and monitoring.
  • Create a digital record (crop diary) for every field, useful for loans and insurance.

The long-term goal is to build climate-resilient, profitable and smart farms that can be scaled to an entire district or state.

5. Expected Impact of the Solution

For Farmers

  • 10–25% potential increase in yield (depending on crop and region).
  • Reduction in water usage through smart irrigation schedules.
  • Lower risk of crop failure due to timely pest and disease alerts.
  • Better market price by improving quality and grading of produce.

For Government

  • Real-time view of crop health at village, block and district level.
  • Better targeting of subsidies (fertilizer, irrigation, seeds).
  • Support for crop insurance, disaster relief and policy making.
  • Digital database of fields, crops and farmer adoption of technology.

For Environment

  • Reduced fertilizer runoff and lower groundwater pollution.
  • More efficient water use, especially in drought-prone areas.
  • Lower carbon footprint due to optimized input usage.
  • Healthier soil that can support long-term productivity.
